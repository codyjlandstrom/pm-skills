# The Mom Test: Detailed Principles Reference

This document provides the detailed framework for evaluating and generating
customer discovery work. The SKILL.md references this file; read it fully before
operating in any mode.

## 1. The Three Rules

**Rule 1: Talk about their life, not your idea.**
The moment you mention your idea, the conversation shifts from learning to
pitching. Even framing a question around your concept ("If we built a tool
that...") contaminates the response. Good questions are about the customer's
world, problems, workflow, and behavior regardless of whether your product
exists.

**Rule 2: Ask about specifics in the past, not hypotheticals about the future.**
"Would you use X?" is a question about an imaginary future. Humans are terrible
at predicting their own behavior. "When did you last encounter this problem?" and
"What did you do about it?" are questions about real events with real answers.
Past behavior is the single best predictor of future behavior.

**Rule 3: Talk less, listen more.**
If you're doing more than 30% of the talking in a discovery conversation, you're
pitching, not learning. The goal is to get the other person telling stories about
their life. Stories contain details, emotions, and specifics that structured
answers don't.

## 2. Question Anti-Patterns

These are the failure modes to watch for when reviewing or generating questions.

### The Hypothetical
- Pattern: "Would you...", "Do you think you might...", "If you could..."
- Why it fails: People are optimistic about their future selves. They'll say yes
  to things they'll never actually do.
- Fix: Convert to past tense. "Have you ever..." or "When was the last time..."

### The Leading Question
- Pattern: "Don't you think it would be better if...", "Wouldn't it help to..."
- Why it fails: You've telegraphed the answer you want. Social pressure does the
  rest.
- Fix: Ask open-ended questions. "How do you currently handle..." or "Walk me
  through what happens when..."

### The Opinion Request
- Pattern: "What do you think about the idea of...", "How do you feel about..."
- Why it fails: Opinions are cheap and plentiful. They cost the customer nothing
  to give and tell you nothing about behavior.
- Fix: Replace opinions with behavior. "What are you currently doing about..."

### The Compliment Trap
- Pattern: Any question where "That sounds great!" is a possible answer
- Why it fails: If the customer can satisfy your question with enthusiasm alone,
  you've learned nothing.
- Fix: Structure questions so the answer requires specific, verifiable details.

### The Premature Solution
- Pattern: "What if we built...", "Would a feature that does X help?"
- Why it fails: You've stopped learning and started selling. The customer is now
  reacting to your solution, not describing their problem.
- Fix: Stay in problem space. "Tell me about the last time X happened."

### The "Anybody" Question
- Pattern: "Do you know anyone who...", "Is there a market for..."
- Why it fails: Asking someone to speculate about other people's behavior
  multiplies the unreliability.
- Fix: Ask about *their* experience specifically. Generalize later from many
  specific data points.

## 3. Question Patterns That Work

### The Anchor Question
Start with a broad, non-threatening question that establishes context.
- "Walk me through how you [relevant activity] today."
- "Tell me about the last time you [encountered this situation]."
- "What does a typical [relevant time period] look like for you?"

### The Dig
When the customer mentions something interesting, dig into the specifics.
- "Can you tell me more about that?"
- "What happened next?"
- "How much time/money did that end up costing?"
- "What was the hardest part about that?"

### The Emotional Probe
Emotions signal where pain is real, but you need to anchor them to behavior.
- "You mentioned that was frustrating. What did you do about it?"
- "How big of a deal is that in the context of [their priorities]?"

### The Process Question
Understanding existing workflows reveals pain points and switching costs.
- "Walk me through exactly how you handle [process] from start to finish."
- "Where does it break down?"
- "What tools are you using for that right now?"

### The Spending Question
Money is the strongest signal of all. If they're already spending on the problem,
it's real.
- "What are you currently spending to solve this?"
- "Have you looked into any solutions? Which ones?"
- "What happened when you tried [alternative]?"

### The Priority Question
Separating real priorities from nice-to-haves.
- "Where does this rank among the things you're trying to fix right now?"
- "What would happen if you didn't solve this for another six months?"
- "Who else in your org cares about this?"

## 4. The Deflection Taxonomy

When analyzing conversations, these are the categories of non-answers that feel
like answers but contain no real information.

### Compliments
- "That's a really cool idea!" / "I love it!"
- Signal value: zero. A compliment is a social reflex, not a purchase order.
- Recovery question: "Thanks! But tell me, how are you handling this today
  without it?"

### Feature Requests
- "It would be great if it could also do X."
- Signal value: low unless anchored to current behavior. Feature requests from
  people who haven't committed are wishlists, not requirements.
- Recovery question: "Interesting, how are you handling X currently? How
  important is it relative to [core problem]?"

### Generic Enthusiasm
- "Yeah, I'd definitely use that." / "We'd pay for that."
- Signal value: very low. Future-tense enthusiasm is nearly meaningless.
- Recovery question: "What are you currently paying for the thing closest to
  this?" or "What's the budget approval process look like for a tool like this?"

### The Redirect
- "You should really talk to [person/department]."
- Signal value: moderate. It might mean they're not the right person (useful
  info), or it might be a polite way to end the conversation.
- Recovery question: "Definitely, I'd love an intro. But from your perspective,
  how does this affect your work day to day?"

### The Hedge
- "Maybe..." / "It depends..." / "In certain cases..."
- Signal value: moderate if you dig in, low if you don't.
- Recovery question: "Can you give me a specific example of when it would and
  when it wouldn't?"

## 5. Commitment and Advancement

A conversation's value is measured by the commitment you receive at the end, not
by how encouraging it felt.

### Commitment Ladder (weakest to strongest)

1. **Nothing** - "Great chat, keep me posted." (No value. This is a polite
   dismissal.)
2. **Time** - Agreeing to a follow-up meeting with a specific date. (Low but
   real. They're investing something.)
3. **Reputation** - Introducing you to someone else, especially a decision-maker
   or a peer with the same problem. (Moderate. They're putting their name on it.)
4. **Money** - A letter of intent, a pre-order, a deposit, or an actual payment.
   (Strong. Money is the only unambiguous signal.)
5. **Effort** - Agreeing to run a pilot, providing access to their data, or
   dedicating engineering time to an integration. (Strong. Effort is expensive
   and demonstrates real pain.)

If you leave a conversation with nothing higher than level 1, you have not
validated anything, regardless of how positive the conversation felt.

### Offered vs. Realized Commitments

An offered commitment ("I'll intro you to our CISO") is not the same as a
realized commitment (the intro email lands in your inbox). Many offered intros,
follow-ups, and meetings never materialize. When analyzing a conversation, score
offered commitments one level lower than realized ones on the ladder above.
"He offered to connect me with the CISO" is not a Level 3; it's a Level 2 until
the email is sent. Track whether offered commitments convert, because the
conversion rate itself is a signal: a prospect who consistently follows through
is demonstrating real interest, while one whose offers evaporate is giving you
polite dismissals dressed up as advancement.

### How to push for advancement

- "What would it take to try this with your team?"
- "Would you be willing to [specific concrete thing] if we [specific concrete
  thing]?"
- "Who else on your team should I be talking to? Could you make an intro this
  week?"
- "If we had a working version in [timeframe], would you be willing to run a
  pilot?"

## 6. Conversation Planning

Before any discovery conversation, clarify:

1. **What's the riskiest assumption?** This is the one thing you need to learn.
   Everything else is bonus.
2. **What would falsify it?** If you can't define what a bad answer looks like,
   you can't learn from the conversation.
3. **Who is the right person to talk to?** The answer needs to come from someone
   who actually has the problem, makes the decision, or controls the budget.
   Talking to the wrong person wastes both your time.

After the conversation, ask:

1. **What did I learn that I didn't know before?** If the answer is "nothing
   concrete," the conversation failed regardless of how pleasant it was.
2. **Did I get a commitment?** See the commitment ladder above.
3. **What's my next riskiest assumption?** Discovery is iterative. Each
   conversation should narrow the list.

## 7. Signal vs. Noise

### Strong Signals (trust these)
- They describe a specific past behavior in detail
- They've already spent money or significant time trying to solve the problem
- They've built internal workarounds
- They agree to a concrete next step without being pushed
- They spontaneously bring up the problem before you ask about it
- They get emotional about a specific incident (combined with evidence of action)

### Weak Signals (verify these)
- They say they'd pay but haven't looked at alternatives
- They describe the problem as important but rank other things higher
- They're enthusiastic but can't describe their current workflow in detail
- A manager says the team needs it, but individual contributors don't mention it

### Noise (discard these)
- "That's a great idea!"
- "I'd definitely use that."
- "You should charge more!"
- "Everyone I know would want this."
- Any statement about what they "would" do in a hypothetical scenario
- Compliments on your pitch, demo, or presentation

### Interviewer Bias (flag in conversation notes)
When analyzing notes or transcripts, watch for the interviewer's own
editorializing mixed in with customer data. These are feelings, not facts:
- "She seemed really engaged the whole time"
- "He was clearly excited about the idea"
- "It was a really positive call"
- "I could tell she was interested"
- "The energy in the room was great"

These are dangerous because they inflate perceived signal. A call can feel
amazing and yield zero trustworthy information. When you see these in notes,
flag them explicitly and redirect attention to what was actually said and what
commitments were actually obtained.
